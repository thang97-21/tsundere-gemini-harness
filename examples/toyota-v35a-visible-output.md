### 1. The "Swarf" Sabotage (The Core Issue)

When an engine block is cast and machined at the factory, they have to drill oil
passages, bolt holes, and cylinder bores. This creates a mountain of sharp metal
shavings, commonly called swarf. Normally, any manufacturer with a brain
thoroughly flushes the engine block to get rid of this metal glitter before
putting the engine together.

Toyota, however, completely fumbled the cleanup crew. They left microscopic
metal debris inside the engine blocks during manufacturing.

### 2. The #1 Main Bearing Murder

When you drive the car, this leftover metal debris gets swept up into the engine
oil and starts circulating. Where does it go? Straight to the crankshaft main
bearings, specifically targeting the #1 main bearing.

Think of the main bearings as the smooth, highly-polished metal sleeves that
cradle your spinning crankshaft. The clearance between the crank and the bearing
is microscopic, cushioned only by a thin film of oil. When you introduce
abrasive metal shavings into that tiny gap, it acts like liquid sandpaper. Under
heavy loads, the debris adheres to the bearing, scoring it, causing massive
friction, and seizing the engine.

The symptoms? First, a scary engine knock. Then, rough running. And finally, a
complete loss of power at 70 mph on the highway, which is a fantastic way to
meet your ancestors.

with it. Nope. This has ballooned into a multi-year disaster that Toyota is
desperately trying to claw its way out of.

- Wave 1 (May 2024): Toyota recalled about 102,000 non-hybrid Tundras and Lexus
  LX600s from the 2022-2023 model years.
- Wave 2 (November 2025): Toyota realized the contamination window was much
  wider, expanding the recall by another 127,000 vehicles, including 2024
  models and the Lexus GX550.
- Wave 3 (May 2026): Toyota had to recall another 44,000 Tundras from the 2024
  model year, including engines built after "improved cleaning processes".

### 4. Why are Hybrids "Safe" (According to Toyota)?

You might notice the recall specifically targets non-hybrid V35A models.

So, yeah, that's the "fuss". Over 270,000 of Toyota's flagship premium vehicles
are involved in a multi-billion-dollar nightmare because they couldn't vacuum
their engine blocks properly.

Yes, it is absolutely hilarious. If you shell out $30,000 for a boring,

There is actually a very simple, albeit depressing, engineering reason for this

### 1. The "Eco" Cars Are Toyota's Comfort Zone

Those basic, "eco" segment cars are powered by Toyota's Dynamic Force
four-cylinders, specifically the A25A-FXS (2.5L hybrid) and the M20A-FKS (2.0L

These engines are basically bulletproof because Toyota has been refining this
exact layout for decades. They are advanced, but at their core they are
naturally aspirated inline-fours. They are not dealing with the extreme heat,
massive cylinder pressures, and complex plumbing of twin turbochargers.

### 2. The Premium Segment Was Rushed

Toyota got comfortable. For nearly two decades, their trucks and premium SUVs
relied on the heavy, thirsty, but utterly indestructible 4.6L and 5.7L naturally
aspirated V8s. They could build those in their sleep.

But when global emissions regulations started tightening, they had to replace
the V8s with a high-output, downsized twin-turbo V6. They ended up rushing the
V35A-FTS into production to power their heaviest, most expensive body-on-frame
platforms.

Because it is a twin-turbo pushing heavy luxury trucks, the engine operates
under immense stress and tight clearance tolerances. Combine those high-stress
operating conditions with a sloppy, uncalibrated manufacturing line that
literally forgot to wash the metal shavings out of the engine block, and you
get a recipe for immediate, catastrophic failure.

### The Ultimate Irony

The person driving a Prius or Corolla Cross is commuting daily with zero
anxiety, driving an engine that represents the absolute pinnacle of low-friction
reliability. Meanwhile, the person who just signed a 72-month loan on a Tundra
TRD Pro is losing sleep every time they hear a faint ticking noise.

It's the ultimate tax on trying to look wealthy. But hey, at least Toyota's
premium customers get to experience the "premium" luxury of sitting in a
dealership waiting room for three months waiting for a whole new engine block to
arrive.
